Strand bias analysis in NGS data

In recent years, next-generation sequencing (NGS) has brought significant progress to genomic research and diagnostics. However, NGS is still prone to many errors, and one of them is strand bias. This page provides interactive results of strand bias analysis in datasets of three major sequencing technology companies: Illumina, Pacific Biosciences and Oxford Nanopore Technologies.

Strand Bias

Strand bias is a situation when one DNA strand is favoured over the other strand. Due to the varying amount of evidence favouring one gene variant compared to another, there is a possibility of an incorrect evaluation of the result. Knowing the correct frequencies of gene variants is crucial for population genotype analyses. It is also an important factor in medicine because frequencies of gene variants identify numerous genetic disorders. In theory, the incorrect evaluation of results could lead to inaccurate diagnosis and, therefore, wrong treatment.  

In bioinformatics, k-mers refer to all possible substrings of length k contained in the nucleotide sequence. Reverse complement of k-mer is created by constructing a complementary sequence to it and then reversing the sequence so that the last nucleotide becomes the first. Here we recognise strand bias as varying frequencies of the k-mers and their reverse complements. When both k-mer and its reverse complement have the same number of occurrences, it means this k-mer has no bias. However, when their frequencies vary, it means k-mer has strand bias. The higher the difference between k-mer and its reverse complement, the more concerning the strand bias is.  

In the interactive plots, strand bias is represented by the ratio of less frequent (LF) of the k-mer and its complement towards the more frequent (MF) subtracted from 1 and converted into a percentage. The subtraction from 1 is needed because a ratio represents how much the strings are ‘alike’, while the focus should be on how much they differ.  

SB = [(1 − LF/MF) ∗ 100]
